Sri Lanka Economic Crisis (2015–2025)

A Comprehensive Macroeconomic Analysis

1. Structural Weaknesses (2015–2019)

Sri Lanka entered the latter half of the 2010s with fragile macroeconomic fundamentals. The country ran persistent fiscal deficits above 5% of GDP, and public debt rose from 78% of GDP in 2015 to nearly 94% by 2019. A major blow came in 2019 when sweeping tax cuts reduced government revenue by over 30%. This eroded the government’s capacity to invest or respond to crises.

The economy heavily depended on foreign borrowing—primarily via short-term, dollar-denominated sovereign bonds. Imports far exceeded exports, leading to chronic current account deficits. These combined structural weaknesses left the economy highly vulnerable to any external shock.

2. COVID-19 & Economic Collapse (2020–2022)

The COVID-19 pandemic hit Sri Lanka at its most vulnerable. The tourism sector— a major foreign exchange earner—collapsed from $4.4 billion in 2018 to just $0.5 billion in 2021. Remittances declined sharply, and lockdowns paralyzed domestic activity. GDP contracted by -3.6% in 2020.

In an attempt to defend the rupee without adequate reserves, the government printed money, resulting in hyperinflation (~70% in mid-2022). Foreign reserves dropped from $7.6 billion in 2019 to below $2 billion by 2022. Import bans on essentials like fuel, food, and medicine triggered severe shortages, leading to public unrest.

3. Sovereign Default & Political Fallout (2022)

In April 2022, Sri Lanka defaulted on $51 billion in external debt, marking its first sovereign default. Widespread protests erupted due to inflation, shortages, and economic hardship. This led to the resignation of President Gotabaya Rajapaksa.

The crisis prompted Sri Lanka to seek IMF assistance. A $2.9 billion Extended Fund Facility was secured in March 2023, conditioned on deep structural reforms and fiscal tightening.

4. Recovery Measures & Structural Reforms (2023–2025)

Under IMF guidance, Sri Lanka initiated sweeping reforms. Taxes were reintroduced, subsidies curtailed, and the central bank gained independence. The rupee was floated, allowing market-based exchange rates.

Inflation declined to ~15% by late 2023 and is expected to fall to single digits by 2025. GDP growth recovered mildly to 1.5% in 2023 and is forecasted at 3.5–4% by 2025. Tourism and remittance inflows are gradually recovering, although debt restructuring and fiscal consolidation remain ongoing challenges.

5. Public Debt (% of GDP, 2015–2019)

Debt rose from 78% to 94% of GDP—indicating rising insolvency risk and fiscal irresponsibility.

6. Forex Reserves (USD Billion, 2019–2022)

Reserves collapsed from $7.6B to under $2B, triggering a currency crisis and import shortages.

7. Macroeconomic Concepts Applied

  • Fiscal Deficit: Persistent overspending without matching revenue.
  • Balance of Payments: Chronic current account deficits due to trade imbalance.
  • External Debt: High exposure to short-term, high-interest foreign loans.
  • Monetary Policy: Money printing caused hyperinflation.
  • Exchange Rate Management: Mismanaged peg led to a currency crash.
  • Inflation & Expectations: Prices surged as public confidence fell.
  • Political Economy: Populist policies compromised long-term sustainability.
